Sustainability has to be handled with precision.

The OCP guideline sets an O D P near zero and a G W P below seven hundred. Those are important baseline expectations. The industry keeps watching fluid roadmaps because refrigerant chemistry has changed before, and it will keep changing as environmental regulations and customer requirements evolve.

Some current low-G W P dielectric fluids may still raise P FAS questions. That does not automatically make a deployment unacceptable, but it does make containment, leak response, recovery, disposal, and supplier documentation important. A responsible evaluation should stay precise about what the guideline requires, what suppliers must document, and what operators must manage.

The panel also discussed the possibility that future standards may allow additional fluid classes, such as A two L fluids, in data center contexts. That would give fluid manufacturers another design option, but it would also require careful safety and code review.

The practical takeaway is not to chase a single "perfect" fluid. It is to evaluate the fluid against the project requirements, the regulatory environment, the wetted materials, the operating temperature, and the lifecycle plan.

Low environmental impact, safe handling, and reliable thermal performance all have to be true at the same time.
