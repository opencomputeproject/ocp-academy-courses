# pronunciation: Soxhlet -> "Socks-let"; TCS -> "T C S"; PRV -> "P R V"
The guideline recommends a layered qualification path. It is not enough to dip a material in a fluid once and call the system compatible.

First, identify critical components and wetted materials. The list should include metals, elastomers, plastics, seals, pumps, valves, cold plates, quick disconnects, and heat exchangers.

Second, test material compatibility for the specific dielectric liquid. The guideline describes a seventy-two-hour Soxhlet extraction test. The test measures extraction and absorption behavior, and it is intended to give worst-case results at the fluid boiling point. The guideline also says the sealing weight, stiffness, and volume should not change by more than five percent.

Third, follow positive material results with functional verification. The guideline points to component tests, pump performance, cold plate thermal performance, system performance, component functionality, P R V long-term performance, solenoid function, and full system testing.

Fourth, use monitored system testing. The guideline refers to a two-month monitored functional test in a working system after positive Soxhlet results.

This evidence path protects reliability. A material may look acceptable in isolation but fail as part of a pumped, heated, pressurized, serviced system. Qualification needs to follow the real operating context.
