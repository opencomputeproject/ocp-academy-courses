Compatibility starts with a simple question: what does the fluid touch?

The OCP guideline calls those surfaces wetted materials. They include the obvious parts, such as cold plates, heat exchangers, and tubing. They also include parts that can be easier to overlook, such as pump seals, quick-connect seals, valve seats, buffers, manifolds, service ports, and internal C D U surfaces.

The operator is responsible for certifying compatibility of the heat transfer fluid with the liquid-cooled rack system, including all wetted components. That means the fluid specification is tied closely to the wetted-material list.

If a new material is added, the compatibility basis changes. A seal substitution, connector change, pump change, or new tubing material may require re-evaluation. The fact that a fluid is dielectric does not make every material compatible with it.

The guideline's example tables make this concrete. Some metals and alloys may show no change in the example fluid. Some elastomers and plastics may be acceptable. Others may fail because weight, volume, hardness, stiffness, or surface appearance changes too much.

For a deployment team, the takeaway is to treat the wetted-material list like a controlled engineering artifact. It belongs in design reviews, supplier submittals, maintenance records, and change control.
