# pronunciation: GPU -> "G P U"; GPUs -> "G P Yous"; TDP -> "T D P"; DLC -> "D L C"
High-power chips do not create heat evenly across the die. A G P U can shift work across regions over time, so the hottest area can move with the workload.

Pool boiling is one way to respond to that dynamic heat flux. In this cold plate concept, heat-conducting fins and a wick structure work together. The fins bring heat from the chip into the fluid region. The wick keeps the liquid distributed where boiling needs to happen.

Where heat flux is higher, bubble generation becomes more intense. Where heat flux is lower, bubble generation is lighter. That local response is part of the appeal: boiling can occur where the heat is generated, rather than relying only on bulk fluid temperature rise through a channel.

This does not make design automatic. The surface, the wick, the liquid supply, vapor removal, orientation tolerance, and dryout margin all matter. If vapor cannot leave, or if liquid cannot replenish the hot region, the cold plate can lose performance.

The practical takeaway is that two-phase cold plates are about proximity and stability. They try to put the phase-change event close enough to the die that very high T D P and moving hot spots can be controlled without excessive flow through the server.
