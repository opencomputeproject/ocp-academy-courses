# pronunciation: CDU -> "C D U"; TCS -> "T C S"; FWS -> "F W S"
A controlled two-phase loop has a few familiar pieces, and a few pieces that deserve special attention.

Liquid leaves the C D U side of the technology cooling system and feeds the cold plates. At the cold plate, heat from the device boils part of the liquid. The return line carries vapor, or a vapor-rich mixture, back toward the C D U. There, a condenser rejects heat to facility water, ambient air, or another heat-rejection path, and converts vapor back into liquid.

Many systems include a receiver, accumulator, or buffer that manages charge and liquid inventory. Pumped systems also need the pump inlet to receive liquid with enough subcooling to avoid cavitation. Passive designs move fluid differently, but still must return liquid to the evaporator and remove vapor from it.

The control problem changes because pressure and saturation temperature are linked. If the system controls pressure, it influences the boiling temperature at the cold plate. That is why vapor pressure, vapor temperature, liquid pressure, and liquid temperature become more than monitoring points. They are part of the thermal control story.

At the F W S boundary, the facility still removes a heat load. Inside the T C S, the system must manage phase, pressure, charge, and return liquid.
