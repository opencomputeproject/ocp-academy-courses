Interoperability is where a promising cooling method becomes deployable at scale.

The O C P contribution frames two-phase dielectric cooling around multi-vendor ecosystems, standardized interfaces, material compatibility validation, and reusable liquid cooling infrastructure. That matters because operators do not want each server generation to require a custom thermal world.

Several component categories are candidates for alignment. Q Ds or Q D Cs, manifolds, seals, tubing, cold plates, and C D U interfaces all need clear expectations. The panel points to a desire for connector companies and cooling providers to work toward two-phase versions of the interoperability progress already seen in single-phase liquid cooling.

The hard part is that two-phase designs are not identical. A pumped system, a passive system, a pool-boiling cold plate, and a flow-boiling cold plate may have different needs. But they still cool the same class of silicon, use many of the same server integration points, and depend on components that can be tested and qualified.

For learners, the takeaway is to ask interoperability questions early. Which wetted materials are qualified? Which connectors are approved? Which C D Us can pair with which server loops? Which records prove compatibility?

Those questions reduce lock-in, reduce deployment risk, and keep the ecosystem moving toward reusable infrastructure.
