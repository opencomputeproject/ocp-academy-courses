# pronunciation: CDU -> "C D U"; TCS -> "T C S"; FWS -> "F W S"; DLC -> "D L C"
The C D U is the architectural boundary that lets two different worlds cooperate.

On the technology side, a single-phase C D U typically circulates liquid, filters and conditions that liquid, and rejects heat through a liquid-to-liquid heat exchanger. It modulates pump speed and a facility-water valve to hold a supply-temperature setpoint.

A two-phase C D U has a different job. It receives vapor or a two-phase return, condenses it, manages charge or buffer inventory, and controls pressure so the boiling temperature stays where the design wants it. The heat exchanger on the T C S side behaves as a condenser, not just a symmetric liquid-to-liquid exchanger.

On the facility side, the picture can remain much more familiar. The F W S does not need to know whether the server-side loop boiled a refrigerant or warmed water. It sees a heat load, a supply temperature, a return temperature, flow, and approach temperature.

That separation is a deployment advantage. It means a two-phase row, pod, or rack group can sometimes connect to facility infrastructure designed around liquid cooling, while the C D U absorbs the phase-change complexity.

The boundary is also where requirements become explicit: pressure control, leak containment, materials compatibility, alarms, service ports, and records all need ownership.
