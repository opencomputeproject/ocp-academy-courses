# pronunciation: DLC -> "D L C"; TCS -> "T C S"
The easiest way to understand two-phase D L C is to compare what the coolant does with the heat.

In a single-phase loop, the coolant stays liquid. It enters the cold plate at one temperature, absorbs heat from the device, and leaves warmer than it arrived. The heat is carried as a temperature rise. That is sensible heat transfer.

In a two-phase loop, the working fluid is supplied near saturation. When it reaches the hot surface inside the cold plate, part of the liquid boils. The fluid absorbs heat as it changes phase from liquid to vapor. That is latent heat transfer.

Latent heat is powerful because phase change can carry a large amount of energy without requiring the same temperature rise or the same liquid mass flow. The vapor then has to be condensed back into liquid so the loop can continue.

This is why two-phase cooling is not simply single-phase cooling with a different fluid. The loop has different control variables, different line-sizing concerns, and different failure modes. It also has different opportunities. Lower liquid flow can reduce pumping burden, and near-isothermal boiling can help manage hot spots close to the silicon.

Keep this contrast in mind. Sensible heat asks, "How much did the liquid warm up?" Latent heat asks, "How much liquid changed phase?"
