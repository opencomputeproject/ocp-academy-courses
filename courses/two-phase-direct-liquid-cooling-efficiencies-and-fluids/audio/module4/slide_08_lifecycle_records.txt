Records are what let a liquid cooling system survive changes over time.

The OCP guideline states that the liquid usually has no expiration date, so no routine fluid replacement is expected. When a rack is decommissioned, the dielectric fluid should be drained back into an appropriate liquid container through the designated liquid port. It can be reused in a new system or returned to the manufacturer for refurbishment.

Disposal still has to follow the rules. The guideline points teams to supplier Safety Data Sheets and product labels for regulatory information, and reminds operators that federal, state, provincial, and local requirements may vary.

The submittal record should be comprehensive. It should include identification and source of the heat transfer fluid, documentation of all fluid analyses, records of cooling system components that have been replaced, and a detailed record of the materials of construction for each system.

Those records connect directly back to compatibility. If a seal, tube, pump, valve, or cold plate changes, the team needs to know whether the wetted-material basis still holds.

For operators, lifecycle discipline is what makes reuse credible. The fluid may last, but the evidence has to last with it.
