# pronunciation: CDU -> "C D U"; FWS -> "F W S"; TCS -> "T C S"
Two-phase systems can be deployed in more than one facility pattern.

The most familiar pattern connects the C D U to facility water. In that design, the two-phase dielectric fluid remains on the server side, while the facility water removes heat from the C D U. The F W S can then reject heat through the site's existing or planned primary loop.

Another pattern serves sites without facility water in the data hall. Air-based in-rack C D Us or sidecar condensers can use data hall air to condense vapor back to liquid. That can support row, pod, edge, or retrofit use cases where adding facility water is difficult.

A third pattern moves vapor to external air-based condensers or dry coolers. In favorable climates and designs, this can reduce compressor use and water consumption, because ambient air handles much of the heat rejection.

Deployment can also begin at smaller scale. A row or pod pilot may let an operator test the operating model, service process, monitoring signals, and compatibility evidence before committing to a larger build.

The decision should be site-specific. Ask what infrastructure exists, what density is needed, where water is allowed, what heat rejection path is available, and what operations team can support.
