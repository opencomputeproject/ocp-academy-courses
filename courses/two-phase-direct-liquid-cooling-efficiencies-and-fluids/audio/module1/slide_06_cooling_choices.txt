# pronunciation: DLC -> "D L C"; RDHx -> "R D H X"; TCS -> "T C S"
Two-phase D L C should sit in a portfolio, not on a pedestal.

Air cooling remains attractive where rack densities, chip heat flux, climate, and facility layout make it practical. It has a mature supply chain and a familiar operating model. Rear-door heat exchangers, or R D H X, can extend air-cooled environments by moving heat closer to the rack and reducing room-level airflow stress.

Single-phase D L C is already the mainstream liquid path for many high-density deployments. It places liquid cold plates on high-power components, uses water or water-glycol in the T C S, and connects through familiar C D U and facility-water patterns.

Two-phase D L C enters when heat flux, flow rate, water risk, or pump energy becomes the more important constraint. Pumped systems can support higher loads. Passive or thermosiphon-style systems may trade total rack power for fewer moving parts.

Immersion cooling can also make sense in some niches, especially where full component contact is acceptable and the operational model fits. But it brings different serviceability, retrofit, and ownership questions.

The mature answer is coexistence. The technology choice follows the workload, the site, the density target, and the operator's ability to validate and maintain the system.
