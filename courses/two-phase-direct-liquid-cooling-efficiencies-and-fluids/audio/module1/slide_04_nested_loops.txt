# pronunciation: TCS -> "T C S"; FWS -> "F W S"; CDU -> "C D U"; DLC -> "D L C"
Before comparing cooling technologies, it helps to separate the loops.

Heat begins at the silicon. A cold plate receives that heat from the chip package and transfers it into the technology cooling system, or T C S. In single-phase D L C, the T C S liquid warms as it passes through the cold plate. In a two-phase T C S, a dielectric working fluid is supplied near saturation. It absorbs heat by boiling at the cold plate, returns as vapor or a two-phase mixture, and is condensed back to liquid at the C D U boundary.

The coolant distribution unit, or C D U, is the decoupling point. It lets the technology side use the fluid and controls it needs, while the facility side continues to reject a heat load through familiar infrastructure.

That facility water system, or F W S, may connect to dry coolers, chillers, cooling towers, waterside economizers, or another primary heat-rejection path. From that side, the key question is still load, temperature, flow, and approach temperature.

This boundary matters because it keeps the two-phase complexity mostly inside the T C S and C D U. It also explains why two-phase can be considered in retrofits or mixed deployments without redesigning every upstream facility loop from scratch.
