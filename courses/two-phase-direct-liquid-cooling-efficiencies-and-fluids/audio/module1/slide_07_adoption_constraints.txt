Adoption usually happens when an existing approach becomes less practical, not when a roadmap says the market should switch.

For a purpose-built AI training site, the trigger may be rack density. If a G P U roadmap pushes the rack toward loads that strain air cooling or high-flow single-phase cold plates, two-phase becomes part of the design conversation earlier.

For a brownfield site, the trigger may be disruption. The operator may need more compute in the same space, but have limited room for new chillers, limited floor loading, or limited ability to change the primary loop.

For an edge site, the trigger may be operational simplicity. Space is tight. Staff may not be on site. Water may be scarce. Every watt spent on fans and pumps competes with compute. A compact, waterless-in-server system can become attractive if it meets reliability and service needs.

For another operator, the trigger may be lifecycle risk. They may need reusable infrastructure, compatible components, and a path to future chips without redesigning every rack.

This is why the course avoids a single adoption threshold. The useful question is: which constraint is binding at this site, and does two-phase reduce that constraint more than it adds new ones?
