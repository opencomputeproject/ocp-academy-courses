The reason liquid cooling keeps coming up is simple: compute density is rising faster than the old thermal playbook can comfortably absorb.

For many data centers, air cooling worked because rack power stayed within a manageable range, and the room could move enough air through the servers. As AI accelerators moved into one-kilowatt-class devices, and as more accelerators were packed into each rack, the thermal problem shifted. Cooling stopped being only a facility support function. It became one of the constraints on how much compute could fit, how predictably it could run, and how much energy the data center had to spend moving heat.

That does not mean every site moves directly to two-phase D L C. The more realistic picture is a staged landscape. Air cooling still has a place. Rear-door heat exchangers still have a place. Single-phase liquid cold plates are widely deployed and will continue to be useful. But as chip power, rack density, and heat flux increase, operators start asking whether high airflow, high liquid flow, and tighter water chemistry are still the most practical path.

Two-phase cooling enters that discussion because it changes the physics of heat transport. Instead of only warming a liquid, it uses boiling and condensation to carry heat with much lower liquid flow in the technology loop.
