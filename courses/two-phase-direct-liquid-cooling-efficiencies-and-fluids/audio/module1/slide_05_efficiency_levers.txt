# pronunciation: TCS -> "T C S"; FWS -> "F W S"; CDU -> "C D U"; DLC -> "D L C"
Efficiency in two-phase D L C is not one magic number. It is a set of levers that can add up when the site and workload are a good fit.

The first lever is latent heat. Boiling carries far more heat per unit mass than simply warming a liquid. In a representative comparison, a pool-boiling two-phase case may be near zero point three liters per minute per one thousand watts, while a single-phase case may be around one point five liters per minute per one thousand watts. Treat those as example values, not a universal guarantee.

The second lever is pumping. Lower T C S-side liquid flow can reduce pump energy, pressure-drop burden, tube size, and distribution complexity. It also changes what the C D U has to control.

The third lever is temperature. If the cold plate can hold device temperatures with a warmer heat-rejection path, the F W S may gain more hours where dry coolers or economizer modes can do useful work. That can reduce dependence on compressors or evaporative water, depending on climate and design.

The fourth lever is water placement. A dielectric loop inside the server can keep water out of the white space or out of the server, even when facility water still connects to the C D U.

The practical question is not whether each lever exists. It is whether the deployment captures them without adding unacceptable cost, risk, or operational burden.
