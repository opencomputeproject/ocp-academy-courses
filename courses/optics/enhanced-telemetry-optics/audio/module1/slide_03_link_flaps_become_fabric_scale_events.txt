A link flap is easy to name and hard to diagnose. The switch tells you the link changed state, but the evidence may live in several places. It may be on the switch-facing electrical side of a module. It may be on the optical path. It may be on the host-facing electrical side at the remote endpoint.

That gap gets larger in AI fabrics because link count, traffic coupling, and operational pressure all rise together. A local alarm does not automatically explain whether the local module, remote module, fiber path, host receiver, or a threshold event caused the issue.

The specification responds by pushing a common telemetry model into compliant optics and host software. That gives operators a shared language for link health, not just a collection of vendor-specific debug commands.
This also changes how teams collaborate. A switch software team, optics vendor, qualification lab, and operations team can all look at the same failure and still use different tools. Common telemetry gives them a shared evidence layer.
