The emerging four-core MCF direction is useful because it gives the ecosystem a common target.

A four-core fiber can increase relative path density while keeping the idea simple enough for connectors, fan-outs, and test equipment to converge. That is why multi-source agreement activity matters. The fiber by itself is only one piece of the deployment puzzle.

Near-term deployments may still connect to equipment that expects ordinary single-core fiber. That is where bridge components come in. A fan-in device can map separate single-core paths into a multicore trunk, and a fan-out can map them back at the other end.

The density value is strongest when those transitions are low loss, repeatable, and easy to document. Otherwise the cable gets smaller, but the operational complexity moves somewhere else.

The chart makes the density argument visible. The point is not that every deployment will use the exact diameters or counts shown here. The point is that the physical relationship changes. A multicore approach can carry a much higher core count in a smaller cable envelope than a comparable single-core approach.

That can relieve pathway constraints in ducts, trays, and racks. It can also reduce the number of individual cables that installers have to route and support. But the chart should be read together with the ecosystem caveat. Density savings are most useful when connector loss, fan-out loss, inspection, and documentation stay under control.
