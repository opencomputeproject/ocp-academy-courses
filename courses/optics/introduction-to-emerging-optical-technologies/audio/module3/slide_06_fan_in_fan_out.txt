Fan-in and fan-out devices are transition tools.

Imagine a switch or optical engine with conventional single-core interfaces. Now imagine that the pathway between racks or rows would benefit from a compact multicore trunk. The fan-in maps several single-core paths into the cores of the multicore fiber. The fan-out reverses that mapping at the far end.

That bridge pattern is useful while native MCF interfaces mature. It lets operators gain some cable-density benefit without waiting for every endpoint to change at once.

The tradeoff is that every transition adds an optical and operational consideration. It can add insertion loss, mapping complexity, labeling requirements, and another location to inspect. The bridge is valuable, but it has to be engineered into the link budget and the operations plan.

A useful analogy is a highway interchange. The multicore trunk is the compact highway segment. Fan-in and fan-out devices are the ramps that move traffic between conventional single-core lanes and the denser trunk.

If the ramps are well designed, the transition feels natural. If they are poorly designed, they create congestion and confusion. In optical terms, that means link loss, core mapping errors, difficult inspection, or unclear ownership during troubleshooting.

For early deployments, the bridge architecture may be the difference between a useful pilot and a science project. It allows one part of the ecosystem to evolve before every connected device changes at once.
