Dense optical I/O near silicon makes harness design more important.

CPO and similar architectures can place large numbers of optical lanes close to a switch ASIC or optical engine. Those lanes still have to leave the package, route through the system, and connect to the plant or rack harness.

That is why fiber-array units, pitch conversion, and compact routing modules matter. They are not decorative packaging details. They are the bridge between a dense optical package and a usable fiber infrastructure.

MCF can become part of that story because compact fiber routing is valuable wherever many optical paths converge. But the package, harness, connector, and plant have to be planned together. Density in one layer should not create chaos in the next.

This is also where MCF and CPO begin to reinforce each other. CPO can increase optical I/O density near the ASIC. MCF can help route that density through a smaller physical plant. Harnessing sits between them.

If harnessing is treated casually, the density benefit can disappear into a tangle of fragile fibers. If harnessing is engineered as part of the package and rack system, it can make dense optical I/O more repeatable. That is why fiber-array units, pitch conversion, strain relief, routing modules, and connector access deserve attention in the same conversation as transceiver speed.
