Rack architecture is now part of the network conversation.

Open Rack version three and related rack-scale designs remind us that the optical plant has to share space with power distribution, liquid cooling, airflow, service access, and mechanical retention. A wider rack or a different power shelf changes more than the chassis. It changes the physical routes that fibers can take.

The same is true for liquid cooling. Cold plates, manifolds, hoses, and blind-mate fluid connectors occupy the same service envelope where optical connectors and harnesses need to live.

For AI systems, the best optical design is not just the one with the highest bandwidth. It is the one that works with the rack's power, cooling, and maintenance model. A dense design that is hard to service will still slow the cluster down.

The rack is also where organizations discover that separate engineering disciplines are now coupled. The team responsible for cooling, the team responsible for optics, and the team responsible for service procedures may all be touching the same few inches of physical space.

A rear blind-mate design, for example, is not only an optical connector decision. It has to align with tray mechanics, power busbars, liquid connectors, and the expected replacement motion.

A CPO switch is not only a silicon or optics decision. It changes cooling, cable breakout, monitoring, and field replacement assumptions. That is why rack architecture appears so early in an optics course.
