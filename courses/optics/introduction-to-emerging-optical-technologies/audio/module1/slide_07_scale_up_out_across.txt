The word scale can hide three different design problems.

Scale-up is the tight domain. It connects accelerators inside a node, rack, or rack group. In that domain, packaging, blind mating, connector access, and thermal design can dominate the optical conversation.

Scale-out connects many systems into a larger fabric. Here the pressure shifts toward switch radix, fiber count, routing, link reliability, and the ability to monitor many paths at once.

Scale-across extends the fabric between rooms, halls, campuses, or nearby data-center buildings. In that domain, propagation delay and reach begin to shape where compute can be placed.

These domains overlap, but the technology fit changes. CPO, expanded beam, multicore fiber, hollow-core fiber, and QPU integration are not interchangeable answers. Each one starts from a different pain point.

Scale-up is also where copper limitations show up earliest, because very short, very fast links have to fit inside a constrained mechanical and thermal envelope. Scale-out is where the number of links becomes daunting, because each additional switch tier and fabric connection adds fibers, connectors, and failure points. Scale-across is where distance becomes visible, because a few extra microseconds can matter when the job waits on a collective operation.

These patterns help keep the technology discussion honest. If the problem is service access inside a dense rack, hollow-core fiber is probably not the first answer. If the problem is campus reach and latency, expanded beam is probably not the first answer. Good architecture starts by naming the constraint accurately.
