AI infrastructure changes the optical problem because the network is no longer a quiet utility behind independent applications.

Large accelerator clusters depend on high-speed links that move data between many systems at once. When a training or inference job is tightly synchronized, a link issue can become a job-efficiency issue, not just a network ticket.

In current AI back-end fabrics, 800G is becoming a baseline, with 1.6T deployments ramping. Those speeds multiply the design pressure on switches, faceplates, connector density, and the fiber plant.

That is why this course keeps coming back to the physical layer. The question is not only whether a link can carry more bits. The question is whether the system can route the fiber, cool the equipment, inspect the connectors, recover from failures, and turn up the cluster fast enough to make the investment usable.

A useful way to think about the change is to separate the optical link from the optical infrastructure. A faster transceiver solves the link-rate problem. It does not automatically solve the rack-level routing problem, the connector-cleanliness problem, the maintenance-window problem, or the power budget problem.

That distinction matters because emerging technologies often get compared too loosely. CPO does not solve the same problem as multicore fiber. Expanded beam does not solve the same problem as hollow-core fiber. Each technology is a response to a different bottleneck created by the same larger trend: AI systems need more tightly coupled capacity, and the physical layer has to keep up.
