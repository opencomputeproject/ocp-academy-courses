This timeline adds the quantities behind the previous idea.

Read FE here as front-end, and BE as back-end. The front-end fiber count grows sharply as accelerator generations add more GPUs per rack and more high-speed optical paths. But the back-end network is where the growth becomes especially large.

In the Hopper-era example, the front-end estimate is roughly sixty-four fibers per rack, while the back-end scale-out estimate is roughly two hundred fifty-six fibers. By the Feynman-era example, the front-end estimate has climbed to roughly one thousand one hundred fifty-two fibers per rack. The back-end scale-out estimate has climbed to roughly four thousand six hundred eight fibers.

That is the architectural pressure this course is building toward. The issue is not only faster links. It is the physical reality of thousands of fibers that must be routed, labeled, cleaned, tested, monitored, and repaired without turning the rack into an operations bottleneck.
