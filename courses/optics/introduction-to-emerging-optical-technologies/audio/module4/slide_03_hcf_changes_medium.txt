Hollow-core fiber changes the medium.

Conventional single-mode fiber guides light through solid glass. Light travels very fast in glass, but not as fast as it does in air or vacuum. HCF guides most of the light through an air-filled core, so the propagation speed can be closer to the physical limit.

That is why latency is the headline benefit. The improvement is measured per kilometer, so it matters most when distance is large enough to affect placement or workload timing.

The physics is promising, and recent research has improved the loss story. But deployment still depends on manufacturing scale, connectorization, splicing, bend behavior, test methods, and cost. HCF is a latency tool, but it still has to become an operations-ready cable system.

For infrastructure planning, that evolution matters. HCF is not interesting only because it is faster in principle. It is interesting because the practical performance has been improving enough to make data-center and telecom use cases more realistic.
