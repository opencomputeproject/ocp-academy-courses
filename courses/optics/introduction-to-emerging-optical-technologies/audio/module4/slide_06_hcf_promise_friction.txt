The HCF promise and the HCF friction should be held together.

The promise starts with lower propagation latency. Guiding light mostly through air can reduce delay compared with conventional glass-core fiber. HCF can also reduce some nonlinear effects, which may help high-capacity optical systems.

The friction is about production and operations. Microstructured fiber must be manufactured repeatably. The cable has to tolerate realistic handling. Connectors and splices must be reliable. Inspection and test methods have to produce results that field teams trust.

That is why HCF is best treated as a strong emerging option, not a universal replacement. It may be compelling for latency-sensitive routes before it becomes practical for every link in the data center.

The attenuation chart is useful because it keeps the discussion grounded. HCF has always had an appealing latency premise, but loss behavior determines whether it can be useful over real distances. Modern designs have pushed attenuation down substantially compared with earlier hollow-core structures.

Even so, the chart should not be read as a deployment guarantee. A measured fiber result still has to become a cable product, a connector ecosystem, a splicing process, and an operations playbook. That path from lab performance to deployed reliability is where many emerging technologies either mature or stall.
