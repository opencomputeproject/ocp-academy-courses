A data-center QPU is not useful as an isolated machine off to the side.

The useful model is a hybrid loop. CPUs orchestrate the workflow. GPUs accelerate classical math, control, and some error-correction or decoding work. The QPU executes quantum circuits and returns measurements. The classical system responds quickly and sends the next operation.

That loop is why low-latency integration matters. Calibration, control, and error correction can require fast feedback between classical and quantum resources.

NVIDIA describes NVQLink in this context: high-throughput, low-latency integration between GPU-based accelerated systems and QPUs. The details will evolve, but the architectural idea is stable. Specialized compute needs a fast, reliable system interface to become operationally useful.

The feedback loop also helps explain why quantum integration belongs in an emerging infrastructure course, even if broad deployment is later. The physical system has to support extremely specialized devices while still fitting into data-center operations, scheduling, monitoring, and service models.

That means interconnect is not just bandwidth. It includes latency, determinism, synchronization, and control. The same general lesson from the rest of the course applies here: a promising processor or optical component becomes useful only when the surrounding system can operate it reliably.
