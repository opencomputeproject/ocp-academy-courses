The final lesson is that emerging optics is a portfolio.

Hollow-core fiber targets propagation latency and may expand options for distance-sensitive fabrics. QPU integration targets specialized compute, but depends on fast classical control and a mature software and hardware ecosystem.

CPO, expanded beam, multicore fiber, hollow-core fiber, and QPU integration each relieve a different pressure. Some are about power. Some are about serviceability. Some are about density. Some are about latency or specialized computation.

Adoption depends on more than the headline physics. Packaging, standards, supply chain, testability, reliability, and operations readiness decide when a promising technology becomes normal infrastructure.

That readiness lens is the bridge to the rest of the series. Later courses can go deeper into cable design, installation, cable entry, splicing, panels, connector hygiene, and reliability. Those topics may sound more basic than CPO or HCF, but they are what make advanced optical systems succeed in practice.

Emerging technology does not remove fundamentals. It raises the stakes for fundamentals. The more dense, integrated, or latency-sensitive the system becomes, the more important clean connectors, careful handling, accurate testing, and shared telemetry become.
