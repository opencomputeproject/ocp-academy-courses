Lower latency matters most when distance is part of the performance problem.

In scale-across designs, a data center may need to connect rooms, buildings, or nearby campuses as if they were part of one larger compute system. The farther the endpoints are apart, the more propagation delay matters.

Distributed AI workloads can also be sensitive to slow links. Collective operations may require many accelerators to exchange or reduce data. If one path is slow, the useful work can wait.

Lower latency can also expand siting flexibility. If the practical radius around available power increases, operators gain more options.

But latency is not the only metric. A fiber that is fast but hard to splice, inspect, route, or afford will have limited use. HCF has to win as a deployable system.

A practical example is the boundary between one large campus and several nearby capacity sites. If power, land, or cooling are constrained in one building, an operator may want to place additional compute nearby. The question becomes whether the fabric can behave as a coherent system across that distance.

Lower propagation delay can expand the radius of viable placement, but only within the rest of the system budget. Switching latency, serialization delay, congestion, optics, and application behavior all contribute. HCF helps with one important term in that equation. It does not erase the rest of the architecture.
