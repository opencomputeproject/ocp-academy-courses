The pluggable model has served data centers well because it is modular and serviceable. A technician can replace a failed optic without replacing the switch. That is still valuable.

The challenge is that the model gets harder as lane speeds, port counts, and fiber density rise. High-speed electrical signals have to travel from the switching ASIC to the front panel. That path needs careful signal integrity work, and often more power for signal conditioning.

At the same time, the faceplate is crowded. Traditional cages, airflow paths, cable bend radius, and service access all compete for the same space.

Near-packaged and co-packaged optics move the optical conversion point closer to the silicon. That can reduce electrical reach and open new density options, but it changes service assumptions. The system becomes more efficient, but the repair model must be redesigned with it.

This does not mean pluggables are obsolete. It means the pluggable model has a domain where its advantages are strongest and a domain where its costs become more visible. Modularity, vendor flexibility, and field replacement are powerful benefits. But the higher the bandwidth and the denser the faceplate, the more the electrical path and mechanical envelope matter.

For learners, the key is to avoid a false binary. The industry is not simply moving from good pluggables to better CPO. It is exploring a range of packaging points, because different systems make different tradeoffs between power, serviceability, fiber density, and time to repair.
