CPO is often described as an optics technology, but the strongest argument is system-level efficiency.

First, it can reduce power by shortening high-speed electrical reach. Less electrical distance can mean less signal conditioning and less wasted energy moving bits inside the switch.

Second, less power generally means less heat to remove near dense switching silicon. In high-power racks, thermal headroom is a design currency.

Third, CPO can change faceplate density. If traditional pluggable cages no longer define every port, the system has more freedom to aggregate fibers through denser connector strategies.

Finally, higher optical I/O density can support higher switch radix and larger fabrics. That does not make CPO simple, but it explains why it is so attractive in the AI infrastructure conversation.

The power story is especially important in large AI facilities because power is often the scarce resource. If networking consumes less of the facility budget, more power can be assigned to compute, cooling margin, or growth. Even a small percentage improvement can matter when the deployment scale is very large.

The density story is similar. Traditional faceplates were designed around modules humans can handle one at a time. That model is serviceable, but it consumes space. When switches move toward very high radix and very high optical lane counts, the faceplate becomes a bottleneck. CPO creates room for new fiber aggregation strategies, but only if those strategies are manageable in the field.
