We have just looked at CPO as a package-level change.

CPO moves the optical conversion point closer to the switch ASIC. That can help with electrical reach, power, thermals, and faceplate density. But it does not remove the operational reality at the edge of the system. Fibers still have to be routed. Interfaces still have to be mated. Connectors still have to survive installation, turn-up, cleaning, inspection, and replacement.

That is the bridge to expanded beam optics, or EBO.

EBO is not trying to solve the same problem as CPO. It is focused on the service interface. Instead of concentrating the optical spot at a tiny physical-contact point, EBO uses lensing to expand and collimate the beam. That makes the connection less sensitive to small particles at the mating interface.

So the question shifts from where optics should live inside the switch, to how dense optical interfaces can behave more predictably in the field.
