Co-packaged optics is easiest to understand as a boundary shift.

In a traditional pluggable model, the switch ASIC sends high-speed electrical signals across the board to optical modules at the front panel. In a co-packaged model, the optical engines are placed much closer to the ASIC package.

That shorter electrical path is the main reason CPO can help with power and thermal efficiency. The signal spends less distance in the most difficult electrical domain before becoming optical.

The OIF co-packaging work is important because this kind of architecture needs common implementation agreements. The package, electrical interface, optical engine, fiber attach, laser strategy, and management model all need to interoperate well enough for real systems, not just lab demonstrations.

The roadmap visual is helpful because it shows several intermediate steps. Copper direct-attach, pluggable optical modules, onboard optics, 2.5D CPO, chiplet-style CPO, and 3D CPO all move the boundary in different ways.

At each step, the electrical reach tends to shrink and the packaging integration tends to increase. That creates a useful design trade. Less electrical distance can reduce power and improve signal integrity. More integration can make repair, qualification, and multi-vendor substitution harder.

This is why CPO is both attractive and demanding. It is not a connector swap. It is a system architecture decision that affects silicon packaging, optical coupling, thermal design, manufacturing, monitoring, and the field service model.
