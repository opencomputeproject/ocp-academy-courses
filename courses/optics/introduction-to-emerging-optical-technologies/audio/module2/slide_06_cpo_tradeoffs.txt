The tradeoffs are just as important as the benefits.

Serviceability is the first question. A pluggable optic is easy to remove. An optical engine near the ASIC may not be. The system needs a repair model, a sparing model, and diagnostics good enough to identify the failed element.

Laser strategy is another question. Designers have to decide whether light sources are integrated, remote, or external, and how those choices affect reliability.

Thermal coupling also changes. Optical engines are now close to high-power silicon and liquid-cooled structures.

Then there is the ecosystem problem: form factors, fiber attachment, management, manufacturing yield, and vendor interoperability all need to mature together.

CPO is promising because it attacks real system constraints. It is challenging for the same reason: it moves optics into the heart of the system.

Qualification also changes. A pluggable module can often be tested as a replaceable unit with a familiar management interface. A more integrated optical engine may require tighter coordination between the switch vendor, optics supplier, package design, thermal solution, and host software.

The monitoring model has to mature too. If an optical function is harder to replace physically, the system must be better at telling operators what has failed and where. Better telemetry becomes part of the serviceability answer. That connection matters later in the series, especially when we discuss optics reliability and enhanced telemetry.
