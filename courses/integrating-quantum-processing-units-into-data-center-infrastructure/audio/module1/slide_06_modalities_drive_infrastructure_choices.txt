# pronunciation: QPU -> "Q P U"; QPUs -> "Q P U systems"; NV -> "N V"; PIC -> "P I C"; PICs -> "P I C systems"
Quantum systems are built from different physical approaches, called modalities.

The whitepaper discusses superconducting circuits, photonic systems, trapped ions, neutral atoms, semiconductor spin systems, and nitrogen-vacancy centers in diamond. Each modality realizes quantum information differently. That physics choice becomes an infrastructure choice.

Some systems need dilution refrigerators and millikelvin operating temperatures. Some depend on highly stabilized lasers, optical tables, vacuum systems, or low-vibration rooms. Some use telecom-band fiber and look more like rack-level appliances. Room-temperature diamond systems bring a different set of electromagnetic and optical considerations.

The market talk adds another useful lens. Quantum can also be understood through material platforms: superconducting chips, photonics, semiconductors, nanomaterials, and diamond. That matters because ecosystem readiness is not only about Q P U vendors. It is also about suppliers, fabs, thermal systems, control electronics, and facility providers.

This is the first major planning habit for the course: do not ask, "Can this data center host quantum?" as though quantum were one equipment category. Ask which modality, which deployment model, which environmental envelope, and which classical stack. The answer changes the facility conversation immediately.
