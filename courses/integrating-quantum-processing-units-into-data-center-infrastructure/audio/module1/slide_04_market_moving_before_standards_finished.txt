# pronunciation: QaaS -> "quantum as a service"; QPU -> "Q P U"; QPUs -> "Q P U systems"
The market is moving before the standards are finished.

The summit market outlook described twenty twenty-six as a year when more pure-play quantum computing companies were moving into public markets. It also emphasized quantum as a service as an important business model. In that model, hardware providers, facility providers, cloud providers, and end users all have a role in how quantum access becomes usable.

That market attention is useful, but it also creates a risk. If quantum is treated only as a financial story, infrastructure teams may inherit vague promises instead of clear requirements.

The practical question is different: what must a facility provide so a Q P U system can operate reliably, be serviced safely, connect to classical resources, and scale beyond a pilot? This course stays close to that question. It treats market momentum as a reason to standardize, not as proof that every deployment is ready.

For learners, the useful signal is not a single forecast number. It is the shift in roles. Hardware vendors need deployment partners. Facility operators need clearer envelopes. Cloud and platform providers need access models. End users need a way to explore use cases without owning every layer. That is exactly where data-center integration work becomes strategic.
