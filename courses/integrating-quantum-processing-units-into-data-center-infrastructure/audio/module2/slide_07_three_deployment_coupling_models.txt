# pronunciation: QPU -> "Q P U"; HPC -> "H P C"
The whitepaper describes three primary deployment strategies.

In standalone deployment, the Q P U system is housed independently and accessed remotely. This can simplify the first installation, but it introduces higher latency for hybrid quantum-classical workloads.

In co-located deployment, the Q P U system sits near classical compute resources. This reduces latency and supports more efficient hybrid workflows, while keeping the integration challenge manageable.

In on-node integration, the Q P U is embedded directly within a classical compute node, similar in concept to a G P U or other accelerator. This offers the tightest coupling and lowest latency, but it requires the most sophisticated infrastructure and system design.

The vendor defines the deployment strategy, but the facility feels the consequences. Rack layout, power, cooling, networking, orchestration, service access, and environmental controls all change depending on how tightly the quantum and classical systems are coupled.

Use these three models as a first-pass interview tool. If a deployment is standalone, ask how users reach it and what latency means for the workload. If it is co-located, ask what must sit nearby and who owns the interconnect. If it is on-node, ask whether the facility can support the tightest coupling without compromising serviceability or safety.
