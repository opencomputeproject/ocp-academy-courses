# pronunciation: QEC -> "Q E C"; QPU -> "Q P U"
Storage planning changes when error correction data becomes continuous operational telemetry.

The whitepaper gives two striking estimates. Future fault-tolerant quantum computers could generate approximately 100 terabytes per second of error-correction data. It also gives a smaller example: one logical qubit encoded across 1,000 physical qubits, sampled at 1,000 cycles per second, with 4 bytes per sample, produces about 4 megabytes per second of metadata.

That works out to roughly 345 gigabytes per day for one logical qubit. And that is only raw Q E C syndrome data. It does not include calibration histories, drift models, waveform captures, or A I-assisted prediction and control logs.

The operational lesson is sharp. Quantum storage demand scales with qubit-hours of operation, not only with user job output. A quiet-looking workload can still produce a sustained write-heavy stream because the system is constantly observing, correcting, and verifying itself.

This makes observability a storage problem as much as a monitoring problem. Teams may need retention for syndrome histories, decoder states, calibration traces, diagnostics, experiment provenance, audit records, and model-improvement data. Those needs affect storage media, network paths, write endurance, and governance long before a future machine reaches full fault tolerance.
