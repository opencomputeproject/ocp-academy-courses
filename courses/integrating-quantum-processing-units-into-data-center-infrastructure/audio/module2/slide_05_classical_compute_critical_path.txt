# pronunciation: AI -> "A I"; QPU -> "Q P U"; QEC -> "Q E C"; GPU -> "G P U"; GPUs -> "G P U processors"; FPGA -> "F P G A"; GB200 -> "G B two-hundred"; NVL4 -> "N V L four"
Classical compute sits in the critical path for quantum.

The whitepaper separates several classical roles. One role is running the quantum processor itself. Another is A I acceleration for workload mapping, transpilation, queue optimization, post-selection, and error mitigation. A third is low-latency compute for Q E C, where corrective decisions must happen fast enough to matter.

Those roles have different infrastructure profiles. A I-oriented work can align with conventional high-density H P C architectures. The whitepaper's Dell example uses G B two-hundred N V L four in an IR7000 rack series, supports 144 Blackwell G P U processors, is liquid cooled, and can reach up to 264 kW per rack.

Error correction is different. It requires low-latency accelerator resources close to the quantum control path. The design question is not just how much classical compute exists. It is where that compute sits, how fast it communicates, and whether it can keep up with the Q P U system.

That is why quantum-classical system design is still an active area of exploration. Some workloads can tolerate distance between the Q P U system and the classical resources. Others cannot. A facility conversation about quantum therefore needs to include physical adjacency, cabling paths, switch placement, deterministic latency, and whether classical accelerators are part of the tenant system or part of a broader shared environment.
