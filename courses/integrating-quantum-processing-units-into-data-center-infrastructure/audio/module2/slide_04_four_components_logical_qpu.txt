# pronunciation: QPU -> "Q P U"; QPUs -> "Q P U systems"
The logical Q P U reference stack gives infrastructure teams a useful map.

The first component is the real-time host. It receives error signals, calibration and health data, and logical instructions. It decides how to correct errors, adjust control settings, and schedule operations.

The second component is the real-time interconnect. It carries those decisions between classical control resources and the quantum system controller with the timing needed for reliable operation.

The third component is the quantum system controller. It converts instructions into precisely timed microwave, optical, electronic, or other control signals.

The fourth component is the physical Q P U. This is where quantum information is prepared, manipulated, and measured.

The whitepaper is careful here: not every system expected to deploy in the next eighteen to twenty-four months needs every part of this reference stack. The stack is a planning model, not a claim that all deployments look identical.

That distinction matters. A near-term photonic appliance, a superconducting system with a dilution refrigerator, and a future fault-tolerant system can have different levels of coupling and different support needs. The reference stack helps teams ask the right questions even when the product details vary.
