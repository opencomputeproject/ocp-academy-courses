Operations must move beyond lab norms.

A laboratory system can depend on expert attention, flexible access, and informal workarounds. A production data center needs clearer expectations: uptime targets, mean time between failures, maintenance intervals, calibration processes, consumable management, access procedures, and replacement workflows.

The whitepaper gives examples across modalities. Superconducting systems may run automated calibration for about 20 minutes a couple of times per day, with full optimizations up to 100 minutes. Annealing systems may have quarterly maintenance. Spin-based deployments can involve weekly in-person service where liquid nitrogen is used, semi-annual preventative maintenance, and longer compressor or pump service cycles.

These are not only vendor details. They affect staffing, access control, change windows, safety procedures, spares, supply chain, and service-level expectations. Production quantum needs an operations model that is written down before the system arrives.

The same applies to calibration. A calibration process that is harmless in a lab may be disruptive in a multi-tenant facility if it changes access windows, workload availability, noise conditions, or power behavior. Treat calibration, maintenance, and consumables as scheduled operational workflows, not as exceptions.
