Shared infrastructure can improve scalability, but it also changes responsibility.

The whitepaper uses Maybell's ColdCloud concept as an example. Instead of every quantum system operating as a fully independent cooling plant, a centralized 4 kelvin plant can support multiple modular millikelvin nodes. Electronics can sit in adjacent racks, and shared services can improve efficiency and scalability.

The architectural idea is broader than cryogenics. It asks what happens when facility resources support multiple quantum nodes in a coordinated way, rather than treating each deployment as an isolated product.

There is a tradeoff. Shared plant resources can improve performance per unit of energy and reduce duplication, but they can also reduce independence and complicate redundancy. The whitepaper notes that this design currently sits within the system integrator's demarcation rather than the facility's.

That boundary may evolve as the market matures.

For now, the safe interpretation is to design for clarity. If a shared plant serves multiple quantum nodes, teams must know who owns the plant, who monitors it, who responds to faults, how maintenance affects each node, and what redundancy exists. The more shared the infrastructure becomes, the more important standard interfaces and operating procedures become.
