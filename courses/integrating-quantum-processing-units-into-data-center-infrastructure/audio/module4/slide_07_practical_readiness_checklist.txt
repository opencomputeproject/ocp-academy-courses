A practical readiness checklist turns the course into a planning conversation.

Start with the modality. What physical implementation is being deployed, and what operating envelope does it require? Then locate the deployment model. Is it standalone, co-located, or on-node?

Next, define the site envelope: geography, vibration, E M I, climate, delivery path, floor loading, staging, room size, power, water, cooling, and network proximity. Define the service boundary. Who owns cryogens, filters, vendor access, calibration windows, environmental measurements, and incident response?

Then review the classical stack. What control-plane resources, H P C or A I resources, storage, timing, observability, and networking does the Q P U system need? Finally, document operations: maintenance intervals, consumables, uptime expectations, safety procedures, and escalation paths.

The checklist should end with the O C P workstream. If a requirement is unclear, that is exactly the kind of gap the community can help standardize.

A good checklist does not force every modality into the same answer. It forces every deployment to answer the same categories of questions. That is how the industry moves from anecdote to practice: not by pretending all Q P U systems are identical, but by making differences visible, comparable, and operationally actionable.
