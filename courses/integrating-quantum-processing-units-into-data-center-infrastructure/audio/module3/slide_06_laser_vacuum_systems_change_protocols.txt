Laser and vacuum systems change room protocols.

Neutral atom and trapped-ion systems often rely on lasers, optical paths, vacuum systems, and stable environmental conditions. The whitepaper recommends practical controls such as clean tents during installation, sticky mats, restrictions on food and shedding materials, H E P A vacuuming, surface cleaning, particle counting, and V O C-controlled procedures.

The facility examples are concrete. A trapped-ion deployment cites hallway width of 1.5 meters, doorway width from 1.2 to 2 meters, a 750 kilogram maximum heaviest component, a minimum setup area of 40 square meters, and 27 kW inclusive of cryogenic infrastructure.

A neutral atom example cites 10 kW, setup areas from 40 to 70 square meters, temperature and humidity constraints, and vibration targets. These are not ordinary server procurement details. They are operating-envelope requirements that need facility, vendor, and operations alignment before delivery day.

The operational pattern is familiar, even if the equipment is new. Treat the room protocol as part of the system design. If optics collect dust, if air conditioning drifts, if nearby equipment adds vibration, or if access procedures allow uncontrolled activity near sensitive hardware, performance and maintenance burden can suffer. Facilities teams become part of the reliability model.
