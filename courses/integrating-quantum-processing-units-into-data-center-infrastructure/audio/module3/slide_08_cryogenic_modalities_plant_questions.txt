# pronunciation: QPU -> "Q P U"; QPUs -> "Q P U systems"; mK -> "millikelvin"; RH -> "R H"; D-Wave -> "D Wave"
Cryogenic modalities bring plant-level questions.

Superconducting, annealing, and silicon spin systems commonly use dilution refrigerators to reach millikelvin temperatures. Superconducting gate-based Q P U systems operate at 10 to 20 millikelvin. Annealing systems can operate below 20 millikelvin. Silicon spin systems may operate from millikelvin temperatures to a few kelvin, depending on implementation.

The facility table shows why this matters. A superconducting example cites a heaviest component of 750 kilograms, 1000 kilograms per square meter floor support, 30 kW peak electrical details, 20 C to 25 C temperature, 25 to 60 percent R H, a vibration value of 400 um/s, and water pressure, temperature, and flow constraints.

Operations also matter. Superconducting calibration is about 20 minutes and typically runs a couple of times a day. Full optimizations can last up to 100 minutes. Some annealing and spin-based systems cite around 6 liters per day of liquid nitrogen where cold traps or larger-scale deployments use it.

Power interruptions are another planning issue. The whitepaper notes that unstable or interrupted power can force recalibration, disrupt environmental control, and degrade operational stability. Some platforms may require 48 hours or longer before returning to operational readiness. That makes backup power, restart procedures, and component-level redundancy part of the deployment discussion.
