Service boundaries need more precision for quantum deployments.

In a standard data center, the facility usually provides access control, cooling, networking, power, and physical space. The tenant controls the equipment inside the rack or cage. That boundary works well when the equipment behaves like conventional I T infrastructure.

Quantum systems can cross that boundary. Some require liquid nitrogen handling, cryogenic service, filter replacement, vendor-only maintenance, special room access, water connections, vibration isolation, or environmental controls that affect more than one rack.

The whitepaper notes that in supercomputing centers, the tenant and landlord can be the same organization. Requirements can evolve organically. In general-purpose data centers and A I factories, those responsibilities are split. That makes demarcation a deployment requirement, not a paperwork detail.

Clear boundaries protect uptime, safety, troubleshooting, and long-term serviceability.

A useful boundary document should name both the physical handoffs and the operational handoffs. Physical handoffs include power feeds, water connections, floor space, shielding, cabling pathways, and access zones. Operational handoffs include who responds to alarms, who performs scheduled service, who can enter the area, and how maintenance is coordinated with facility change control.
