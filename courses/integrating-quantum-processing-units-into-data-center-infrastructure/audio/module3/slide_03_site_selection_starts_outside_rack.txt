Site selection starts outside the rack because the external environment sets the baseline.

The whitepaper calls out regional seismic activity, ground-borne vibration, electromagnetic noise, and climate variability. Nearby rail lines, subway systems, heavy truck routes, building mechanical equipment, transformers, and radio sources can all matter, depending on the modality.

Traditional data center criteria still apply. The site needs reliable power, cooling capacity, network connectivity, access paths, loading docks, elevators, door widths, staging areas, and floor loading. Quantum adds another layer: environmental characterization before the system arrives.

Vendors and environmental original equipment manufacturers often guide that assessment. They measure vibration spectra, magnetic fields, acoustic profiles, thermal stability, humidity, and related conditions. The goal is not perfection. The goal is to know whether the site can hold the operating envelope the system needs.

This assessment should happen early because many mitigations are expensive after the fact. It is easier to choose a quieter zone, plan a delivery route, reserve staging space, and avoid nearby high-power equipment than to retrofit around a system that already arrived. In quantum deployment, the cheapest risk reduction is often better site knowledge.
