# pronunciation: QPU -> "Q P U"; NV -> "N V"
The modality matrix is not a ranking table. It is a planning tool.

Each row describes a family of quantum systems and the kinds of facility care it may need. The columns ask operational questions: does the system need cryogenics, how sensitive is it to magnetic fields, vibration, humidity, and temperature, what power draw should be expected, and are there auxiliary requirements?

The power examples make the spread visible. The whitepaper lists neutral atoms at 3.5 kW to 10 kW, room-temperature N V centers at 0.3 kW to 0.6 kW, photonics at 3 kW, and several cryogenic modalities at 15 kW to 30 kW.

Those numbers are not the whole story. A lower-power system may still need optical stability. A higher-power system may be easier to site if its shielding and service procedures are mature. The operator's job is to translate each row into power, cooling, space, access, risk, and responsibility.

The table also helps avoid false equivalence. Two systems may both use cryogenics, but one may need a dilution refrigerator, another may use a smaller closed-cycle cryogenic subsystem, and another may use cryogenics only for a detector. Those differences change water needs, compressor placement, service intervals, restart behavior, and what the facility must monitor.
