The case for a dedicated specification comes down to three trends. First, latency. The model developer wants to treat the entire scale-up domain as a single huge accelerator, and that abstraction only holds if the network never makes the compute wait. Smaller messages, tighter synchronization barriers, and larger memory domains — every one of these pushes latency budgets down. Foundation models like Llama, GPT-OSS, and DeepSeek don't tolerate flaky fabrics. Second, performance. G P Yous are expensive, and any cycle they spend idle is money on the floor. Highly tuned kernels are fusing data movement with compute, which means the network has to be more deterministic and more efficient. Every byte of protocol overhead is a byte that didn't carry a tensor. Lower overhead means higher goodput, which means more useful compute per accelerator. And third, stability. As scale-up domains grow from eight G P Yous to a thousand and more, the blast radius of a small problem gets larger fast. The spec gives a concrete example: a twenty-percent bandwidth drop in a one-hundred-twenty-eight accelerator domain translates to roughly a five-percent end-to-end performance loss — and that's at small scale. Operators feel that as additional training time and additional cost per token. "ee-sun"'s answer is not a new transport, and not a proprietary fabric. It's an evolutionary set of changes to Ethernet itself — small enough to ride existing optics and switches, focused enough to fix the specific things that make standard Ethernet a poor fit for tightly coupled accelerator clusters.
