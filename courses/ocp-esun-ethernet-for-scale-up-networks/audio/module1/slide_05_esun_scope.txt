"ee-sun" is deliberately narrow. It defines the Ethernet framing and switching behavior for scale-up — and nothing else. The host transport stays out of scope. Application-layer concerns stay out of scope. Anything that isn't Ethernet stays out of scope. That discipline is what makes the specification practical. Accelerator vendors can keep evolving their own transports — OCP S U E, and the various vendor-specific transports for X P U-to-X P U semantics — and the "ee-sun" layer underneath them stays common. The focus areas are four. First, L two and L three framing — what bytes go on the wire. Second, efficient headers — strip the protocol overhead that scale-up doesn't need. Third, error recovery — make the link itself resilient to bit errors instead of pushing every failure up to the transport. And fourth, lossless transport — guarantee that well-behaved traffic never gets dropped inside the fabric. There's a clear demarcation between networking and host functionality, which lets switch and accelerator vendors converge on a common target without dictating accelerator architecture. Version one point zero is intentionally a minimal specification — it establishes the baseline so vendors can build and ship. The OCP working group has committed to backward compatibility as the spec evolves, which protects the investment of the early adopters. Anything the group isn't ready to standardize in version one point zero — for example, fast-evolving transport features being worked on in U E C — stays out of scope until it's stable. The result is a tight, opinionated specification that anyone building an Ethernet scale-up fabric can actually conform to today.
