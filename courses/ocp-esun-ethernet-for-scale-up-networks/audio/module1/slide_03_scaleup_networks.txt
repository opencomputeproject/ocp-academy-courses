A modern AI training cluster runs on three distinct networks, and confusing them is how design discussions go sideways. Start with the front-end network — that's how operators, storage, and the outside world talk to the cluster. It looks like a normal data-center network because that's what it is. Then there's the scale-out network — the fabric that joins racks together, where large jobs spread across hundreds or thousands of nodes for collective operations. And then there's scale-up. Scale-up is the tightly coupled fabric inside what the model developer thinks of as one giant accelerator. It connects the G P Yous, or more generally the X P Yous, in a single domain so they can share memory and exchange tensors at the lowest possible latency. The traffic on a scale-up network is dominated by model-parallel patterns: small messages, frequent synchronization, and no tolerance for jitter or loss. The math is unforgiving. If one G P U stalls waiting on the network, the whole domain stalls with it. So scale-up has to deliver high bandwidth and high goodput, sub-microsecond latency, lossless behavior, and a hierarchical topology that scales beyond a single chassis. Through the Llama four generation, scale-up domains topped out at eight G P Yous in a single node. The next generation is targeting one thousand G P Yous and beyond, spanning multiple racks. That jump — from a backplane to a fabric — is what "ee-sun" is built for.
