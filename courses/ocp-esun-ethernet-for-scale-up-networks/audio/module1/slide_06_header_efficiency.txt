Now the substance: the "ee-sun" Header. Standard Ethernet for I P traffic carries a destination address, a source address, an optional V Lan tag, the EtherType, and then an I P header — twenty bytes for I P version four, up to forty bytes for I P version six — before you get to the transport header and the payload. In a general data-center network with thousands of routes and many fan-outs, you need most of what's in that I P header. Inside a tightly coupled scale-up domain, you don't. Routes are static, the L two domain is the whole fabric, and you've already paid a per-byte tax that hurts most when messages are small — which, in model-parallel traffic, is most of the time. "ee-sun" replaces the I P header with a four-byte "ee-sun" Header, identified by a new EtherType called EH-E T. The value of that EtherType is pending assignment from the standards body, but the structural decision is final. Net savings: twenty to forty bytes of overhead per packet removed, four bytes added back. For a small synchronization message, that's a meaningful goodput improvement, and it compounds across the trillions of messages that flow through a real training run. The trade-off is that you give up some of the smart features that lived in the I P header — explicit congestion notification, traffic differentiation through D S C P, and the entropy you used for hashing across equal-cost paths. The "ee-sun" Header is designed to put each of those capabilities back into a smaller, scale-up-appropriate form. We'll walk the fields next.
