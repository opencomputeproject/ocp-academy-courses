The electrical hazard baseline starts with a simple fact.

The white paper cites N F P A 70E, the Standard for Electrical Safety in the Workplace, and notes that an electric shock hazard is present at voltages greater than 50 volts.

Then it raises the data center context. E S S deployments are moving toward operating at 400 to 1500 volts with hot-swap capabilities. That range creates significant risks that must be mitigated.

The operational instruction is equally direct. Only trained personnel should handle, service, or operate E S S using appropriate personal protective equipment and safety supervision. All E S S must be treated as energized equipment unless it has been properly de-energized and tagged for maintenance or disposal.

This framing matters because distributed E S S can make energized equipment feel routine. A rack-level or sidecar system may look smaller than a facility U P S, but smaller does not mean harmless.

The safe assumption is that the E S S is energized until a qualified process proves otherwise.
