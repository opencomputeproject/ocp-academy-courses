The hazard profile goes beyond electrical shock.

Thermal runaway is the issue many people associate with lithium-ion batteries. The Summit discussion noted that recent code changes have been shaped by lithium battery fire events. The white paper then widens the view. Energy storage systems can introduce thermal, gas, explosion, chemical, and electrical hazards. The mix depends on technology, location, and failure mode.

Gas release is one example. During charge, float charge, overcharge, or fault conditions, some systems can release hydrogen, oxygen, toxic gases, or combustible mixtures. Electrolyte leakage can also create acidic, alkaline, or volatile organic compound exposure.

Those possibilities change the design conversation. A facility may need ventilation, gas detection, combustible concentration control, deflagration venting, safe access procedures, or other measures identified through hazard analysis and authority review.

The practical lesson is simple. Hazard analysis cannot stop at fire. A credible ESS review asks what can energize, what can heat, what can burn, what can vent, what can leak, and how the facility will respond.
