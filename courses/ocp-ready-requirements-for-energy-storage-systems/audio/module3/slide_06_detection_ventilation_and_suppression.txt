Detection, ventilation, and suppression have to work as one system.

The white paper notes that ESS areas will typically need smoke detection, thermal imaging fire detection, or radiant-energy sensing, depending on the applicable codes and standards. Battery rooms may also need hydrogen detection or ventilation failure monitoring, with alarms sent to a constantly attended location.

Ventilation is not just comfort cooling. It may be needed to remove gases from the room or enclosure. Critical safety subsystems, including ventilation, may also need backup power where required.

Suppression depends on the hazard and the space. Water-based systems are common in indoor ESS areas where personnel have regular access. Gas-based systems can fit some spaces, but they usually need closed ventilation to work effectively.

The design must avoid contradictions. Ventilation can remove gases, but airflow can also affect fire behavior. That is why fire protection engineering, hazard analysis, and authority review have to evaluate the full system together.
