Row, rack, and sidecar deployments bring energy storage closer to the I T load.

The white paper describes row or rack-level E S S as shared energy buffering for several racks within a row or for a rack itself. A common E S S may connect to a DC busway or rack power distribution unit. That can enable distributed U P S behavior across multiple racks without necessarily involving the electric utility, distribution system operator, or transmission system operator.

This model becomes especially relevant when fast load changes matter. The paper notes recent use of supercapacitors to smooth power spikes from G P Yous and T P Yous. That is a power-quality and ride-through problem, not only a backup-energy problem.

Sidecar systems can also shift where safety review happens. The storage may sit near rack equipment, cabling, airflow, service paths, and occupied technical space. That proximity raises questions about fault domains, isolation, detection, ventilation, maintenance access, and emergency response.

The lesson is not that distributed E S S is good or bad. The lesson is that moving storage closer to the load also moves the hazard analysis closer to the load.
