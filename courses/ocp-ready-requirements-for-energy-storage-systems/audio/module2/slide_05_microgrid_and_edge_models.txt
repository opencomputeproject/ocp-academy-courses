Microgrid and edge models add another layer of coordination.

The white paper describes this use case as sites that operate as hybrid microgrids. They may combine on-site generation, such as solar, fuel cells, or generators, with battery storage for grid support or islanded operation.

In that model, the E S S is not only a backup device waiting for utility loss. It may interact dynamically with local generation assets, site controls, and grid interfaces. The deployment model is usually centralized E S S with controls synchronized to local generation and grid behavior.

This is also where the boundary of the course matters. The white paper focuses on data center deployment requirements. It recognizes that grid-interactive systems may trigger utility, distribution system operator, or transmission system operator requirements, but it does not try to replace those rules.

For learners, the practical takeaway is to classify the grid interaction early. Backup-only E S S and grid-interactive E S S may look similar from a battery cabinet perspective, but their approval paths can be very different.
