Every E S S has to recharge.

The white paper makes a point that can be easy to miss during early design: charging energy is a facility load. If the charging load is unmanaged, it adds to I T load and other power usage at the facility.

That can create several problems. It may exceed equipment limits. It may exceed grid connection limits. It may increase cost. It may also interfere with overall power usage effectiveness and service reliability if the facility has not planned the timing, controls, and capacity for recharge.

This is especially important when the E S S is distributed. A facility may have many storage nodes, each with its own operational logic. If they recharge without coordination, the site can create new peaks while solving a resiliency problem.

Good design treats charging as part of the power architecture. It asks where the energy comes from, when recharge happens, what controls limit the load, and how the plan behaves after an outage or event that has discharged multiple systems.
