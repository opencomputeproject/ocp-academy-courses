# slide 1.4 — content_two_column "Three Things the Network Must Do"
# target: ~95s · ~230 words
# pronunciation: NIC -> "Nick"; NICs -> "Nicks"

So what does the back-end network actually have to do to keep synchronous training moving. The joint paper distills it to three jobs.

First, load-balance. Every flow has to find a path that isn't already carrying another flow at line rate. Hashing a single flow onto a single path — which is the default behavior of E C M P — works fine for general-purpose traffic but breaks down when a small number of huge flows have to share a fabric.

Second, absorb in-cast. Synchronous collectives create deterministic many-to-one patterns: every G P U in a tensor-parallel group sends to one receiver in the same moment. The network has to drain that in-cast without dropping packets and without backing pressure up the tree into unrelated flows.

Third, ride out failures. Optics flap. Transceivers glitch. Switches reboot or freeze. With millions of links across a training fabric, something is always going wrong somewhere. The training job must not even notice — or at worst, briefly slow down and recover by itself.

These three jobs sound modest in isolation. They're hard because they have to hold at the same time, in hardware, on every connection, under sustained line-rate load. Any transport you put into a back-end AI fabric is judged on how well it does all three. Modules two and three trace exactly how M R C earns each one.
