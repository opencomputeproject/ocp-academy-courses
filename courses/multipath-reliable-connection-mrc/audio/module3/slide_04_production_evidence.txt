Now the production data — straight from the Open A I paper.

A seventy-five-thousand G P Yous pretraining job on Cluster A. In the first minute of startup, before background path-mapping had stabilised, fewer than five packets per queue pair were dropped — a loss rate of roughly one packet in twenty-five million. M R C's multipath spraying absorbs that without any operator action.

A fifty-thousand G P Yous run, also on Cluster A. An optical transceiver on a tier-zero switch glitched and flapped all four of its links in rapid succession. Throughput dipped by about twenty-five percent over the minute the flaps continued. Then the transceiver recovered and throughput returned to full speed immediately. No queue pair failed. The job kept training.

During training of a recent frontier model for ChatGPT and Codex, the operators had to reboot four tier-one switches in the fabric. With M R C, they didn't need to coordinate with the teams running the training jobs. The reboots happened. The jobs kept running. The Open A I engineers describe it like this — quote — "When we see a tier-one switch that is not happy, we simply reboot it without concern for routing convergence."

And the broader operational claim from the same paper — quote — "link failures and flapping links between tier-zero and tier-one switches can largely be ignored."

That's the headline result. M R C has changed the operational urgency of network failures in production AI clusters.
