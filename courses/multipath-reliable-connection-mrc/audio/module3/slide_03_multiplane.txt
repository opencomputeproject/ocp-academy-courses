# slide 3.3 — content_two_column "Two-Tier Multi-Plane Topologies"
# target: ~100s · ~240 words
# pronunciation: MRC -> "M R C"; T0 -> "T zero"; T1 -> "T one"; NIC -> "Nick"; NICs -> "Nicks"; Tb/s -> "terabits per second"; Gb/s -> "gigabits per second"; SRv6 -> "S R V six"

M R C unlocks a topology that wasn't practical before. Start with the building block: a datacenter-class switch at fifty-one point two terabits per second. That silicon gives you either sixty-four eight-hundred-gig ports — or, if you break each port into eight links, five hundred and twelve ports at one hundred gig. M R C uses the breakout option.

With those five hundred and twelve ports per switch and only two tiers, a single fabric connects roughly one hundred and thirty-one thousand G P Yous. A conventional eight-hundred-gig design needs three or even four tiers to reach the same scale.

Two tiers means fewer optics, fewer switches, less power, and fewer components that can fail.

The trick is multi-plane. Each Nick's eight-hundred-gig port is broken into eight one-hundred-gig links, each connected to a different plane — a separate, parallel fabric. M R C sprays across all eight planes simultaneously. If a plane fails, the connection rides on the seven that remain.

The numbers make the trade-off obvious. Losing a single tier-zero to tier-one link costs you zero point four percent of a node's capacity in a one-hundred-gig plane. The same link loss in an eight-hundred-gig single-plane design costs three percent. And losing a whole Nick-side optic costs you one eighth of that node's bandwidth — twelve and a half percent — instead of taking the connection down.

This is the topology Open A I's Stargate site at Abilene and Microsoft Fairwater run.
