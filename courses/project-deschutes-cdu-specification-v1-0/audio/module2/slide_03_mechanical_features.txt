A real-world CDU isn't just pumps and a heat exchanger. The quality of its joints, fittings, and filtration determines its long-term reliability, and the Deschutes spec is opinionated about all three.

Piping connections use what the spec calls an optimized mix of Victaulic grooved couplings and Tri-clamp sanitary fittings. The point of mixing them is to minimize integration risk, reduce pressure drop, and simplify maintenance touch points. Victaulic handles the larger structural joints; Tri-clamp handles the higher-tolerance connections around heat exchangers and instrumentation.

Filtration is layered. On the facility, or primary side, a five-hundred-micron strainer protects the heat exchangers from particulate carried in from the building water loop. On the IT, or secondary side, the main coolant stream passes through twenty-five to forty-four-micron filtration. In parallel, a side-stream microfiltration loop operates continuously at roughly three gallons per minute, filtering down to zero-point-two microns — fine enough to remove biological particles that could otherwise colonize the loop over time.

The unit also includes a Spirovent air separator to purge dissolved gases, and a flow-through expansion tank to absorb thermal expansion in the coolant. Importantly, version one dot zero added front-side serviceability to the expansion tank — a direct response to operational lessons from earlier generations.

Hoses are EPDM, with a peroxide cure preferred for long-term elastomer stability. The combined effect of these choices is a unit you can keep running cleanly for years, not just commission cleanly on day one.
