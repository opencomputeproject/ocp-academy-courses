We'll start with the unit's physical envelope.

The Project Deschutes CDU is sixty-five-point-two-five inches wide, by ninety-one-point-four-four inches tall, by forty-two-point-two-nine inches deep. In packaging, the maximum height grows to one hundred inches to accommodate shipping protection — a number that matters when you're planning loading-dock clearance and elevator paths into the data hall.

Dry weight is five thousand three hundred pounds. Once filled with coolant, the unit reaches six thousand nine hundred pounds wet. That's a three-thousand-one-hundred-kilogram footprint that lands on whatever floor structure supports it, so the spec is explicit that data-center owners are responsible for engaging structural engineers to validate static and seismic loading.

Mechanically, the unit is built on a steel frame with casters and mounting brackets, designed to be rolled into position and anchored. Internal piping is stainless steel — chosen for compatibility with both deionized water and PG-twenty-five coolants. Wetted materials are limited to stainless steel, copper, and EPDM rubber on the secondary side. Nickel-plated brass is explicitly not allowed on the secondary loop.

The spec calls out detailed shock and vibration requirements. Operating shock follows IEC six-zero-zero-six-eight, with three-point-five G half-sine impulses on all three axes. Non-operating shock is rated to thirty G. Transportation testing follows ASTM D-four-one-six-nine. The unit is engineered to survive being shipped, rolled, and seated in a real-world data hall — not just to operate cleanly once installed.
