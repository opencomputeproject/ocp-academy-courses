The Project Deschutes specification is not a paper design. It's Google's fifth-generation CDU, informed by more than seven years of running liquid cooling at hyperscale.

Across the previous four generations, Google has deployed well over one gigawatt of internal Deschutes capacity. Operating a fleet of that size produces something money can't buy — detailed, ground-truth data on what actually fails, how it fails, and what to design differently next time.

Project Deschutes was first announced to the industry at the OCP EMEA Summit in twenty twenty-five. At that event, Google released the zero dot eight draft to the OCP community for public review, walked the audience through the design, and invited feedback from suppliers, hyperscalers, and colocation operators alike.

The version one dot zero specification you're studying is the result of that community engagement. Over the months that followed the Summit, the working group incorporated supplier input, design-for-manufacturing refinements, validation-data updates, and operational lessons from Google's own deployments.

Compared with the zero dot eight draft, version one dot zero adds serviceability improvements on the variable-frequency drives, a serviceable expansion tank, refined dimensional drawings, and updated pump reliability data. The full version history is documented on page two of the spec.

But the contribution goes well beyond a parts list. The published document includes the complete bill-of-materials guidance, manufacturing quality requirements, packaging and shipping specs, barcode labeling standards, validation test plans, and field-replaceable maintenance procedures. In short, Google is open-sourcing not just the design, but the operational playbook that lets you deploy and run it reliably.

The intent is simple. Give the entire industry a credible, drop-in starting point — so suppliers can build to a known target, operators have multi-vendor choice, and AI infrastructure can grow without being bottlenecked on bespoke cooling hardware.
