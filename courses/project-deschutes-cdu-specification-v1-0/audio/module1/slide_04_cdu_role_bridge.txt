Let's place the CDU precisely in the cooling architecture, because the rest of the course depends on this picture.

On the primary side we have the Facility Water System, abbreviated FWS — that's the building's chilled or condenser water loop, supplied by the data center's central plant.

On the secondary side we have the Technology Cooling System, abbreviated TCS — that's the dedicated, clean coolant loop that delivers cold fluid directly to the cold plates inside the IT racks.

The Project Deschutes CDU sits between the two. Inside the unit, three parallel liquid-to-liquid heat exchangers transfer heat from the TCS loop into the FWS loop.

The TCS loop is driven by two pumps in an N-plus-one configuration, so one pump can fail and the unit keeps delivering full flow.

On the facility side, a variable-position primary control valve modulates how much chilled water the unit draws from the building — only as much as it needs to maintain a precise, fixed approach temperature.

That approach temperature is a key concept. It's defined as the difference between the cold TCS supply temperature leaving the CDU, and the cold FWS supply temperature entering the CDU. The Deschutes spec targets a three-degree-Celsius approach.

Holding that tight three-degree gap is what allows the CDU to deliver the high IT-side differential pressure — eighty to ninety pounds per square inch — that modern high-density racks require, while still operating efficiently on the facility side.
