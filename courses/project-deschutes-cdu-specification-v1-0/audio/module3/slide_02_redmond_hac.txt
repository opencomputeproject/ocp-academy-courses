The Project Deschutes specification doesn't just define a CDU. It also defines a companion data-center structure called Redmond: the Hot Aisle Containment, or Hack, interface that sits around the racks the CDU cools.

Section eighteen of the spec, beginning on page fifty-three, lays Redmond out in full detail. The design supports shoulder-to-shoulder deployment of IT racks twenty-eight inches wide, up to fifty inches deep, and between eighty-point-seven and ninety-point-five-five inches tall. Differences in rack depth and height are absorbed by the containment plenums, so you can mix rack sizes within a single bay.

The minimum cold aisle width is fifty-four inches. The hot aisle is sixty-eight-point-four inches between rear-door heat exchanger faces, or one hundred two inches between rack faces if no rear-door units are deployed. A repeatable Redmond bay is one hundred twenty-one inches wide and supports four racks per side.

The overall structure height is fourteen feet, or one hundred sixty-eight inches above the finished floor. Critically, that height accommodates most colocation data centers worldwide — making Redmond a drop-in option rather than a facility-rebuild requirement.

Four critical elevations are specified above the floor. At one hundred point five inches, the Deschutes wall manifold pipes. At one hundred fifteen point five, one hundred twenty-seven point five, and one hundred thirty-nine point five inches, three tiers of cable trays. Above one hundred thirty-nine point five inches, the space is fungible — available to the colo for process-chilled-water piping serving rear-door heat exchangers.

Redmond is the missing other half. The CDU delivers coolant; Redmond gives it a defined, repeatable place to live.
