Liquid cooling solves the thermal problem. But high-density racks also need a lot of power, delivered reliably. The Redmond interface specifies exactly how.

Inside the hot aisle, the structure accommodates eight six-hundred-amp bus ducts — four per side. They run the length of the row and deliver power to the racks in what the spec calls a four-makes-three configuration. That phrase deserves a moment.

Four-makes-three means each rack is fed by four independent power feeds, but only three are required to support full load. The fourth feed provides three-plus-one redundancy. Loss of any single feed has no impact on rack operation. Loss of two feeds still allows graceful operation at reduced load.

Each feed supports up to ninety kilowatts per rack-spot, measured at one-second timescales with three of four feeds operational. That's the headroom modern AI racks need without overprovisioning.

Connector hardware uses IEC six-zero-three-zero-nine five-pin tap boxes, rated thirty or thirty-two amps at two-hundred-thirty to two-hundred-forty volts AC, three-phase Wye — a global standard covering EMEA, North America, and Japan deployments. Three-phase tap boxes can be swapped for single-phase ones on a per-rack-spot basis, so legacy hardware can coexist with new high-density loads.

At the facility level, UPS redundancy must at least match feed redundancy — one UPS per feed minimum — and generator redundancy must support the same. The spec is explicit that any single point of failure must be designed out, all the way back to the central plant.

Power and cooling, in Project Deschutes, are co-designed.
