Now let's talk about how you actually buy one of these units — because the supply chain is as much a Google contribution as the design itself.

Google's stated goal is abundant capacity worldwide, with ten-to-twelve-week lead times, at reasonable and competitive cost. To get there, the spec defines a three-tier supplier model.

At the top sits the Approved Vendor List, or A-V-L. There are two A-V-L slots for the full system, with a target four-week lead time and one to three manufacturing sites each. Google works with A-V-L suppliers continuously — sharing forecasts eighteen to twenty-four months out, planning capacity together, opening books on cost, and managing yield and quality side by side.

Below the A-V-L sits the Reserved A-V-L, or R-A-V-L — two more suppliers held in standby. They're qualified to the same functional, quality, and reliability standards. If an A-V-L has a quality excursion, a force majeure event, or simply can't meet exploding demand, an R-A-V-L can be promoted to A-V-L status within a few weeks.

Below that sits the Recommended Vendor List, or R-V-L. Any qualified supplier can earn R-V-L status by building samples to the functional specification, testing to the quality and reliability requirements, certifying with UL, EMC, and applicable safety standards, and submitting a self-qualification package. Google reviews it and grants the designation.

For components — heat exchangers, pumps, VFDs, power supplies, sensors — Google targets two to three suppliers each, with critical components carrying twelve-week lead times.

The model is explicitly designed to grow the ecosystem, not constrain it.
