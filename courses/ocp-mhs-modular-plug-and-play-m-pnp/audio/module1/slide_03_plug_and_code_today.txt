# pronunciation: DC-MHS -> "D C M H S"; DC-SCM -> "D C S C M"; HPM -> "H P M"; BMC -> "B M C"; M-PNP -> "M P N P"

Today's modular servers are remarkably interoperable — at the hardware layer. The DC-MHS specifications already let you take a DC-SCM from one vendor, plug it into an HPM from another vendor, and the electrical handshake just works. That's been demonstrated at OCP summits, multiple times. The catch is firmware. Pulling that off requires the BMC to ship with prior knowledge of that specific HPM. The BMC vendor either co-designs with the HPM vendor, or maintains a tree of per-HPM firmware images. Whichever you pick, you end up with what the workstream calls snowflakes — bespoke firmware loads that don't carry across HPM generations and don't carry across vendors. Plug-and-Code, not Plug-and-Play. The cost shows up in every dimension that matters: time to market for new HPMs, capital and operating expense to integrate and validate, sustainability lost to single-use firmware, and friction for customers who want to bring their own DC-SCM, their own root of trust, or their own systems-management firmware stack into a host built by somebody else. M-PNP exists to retire that cost.
