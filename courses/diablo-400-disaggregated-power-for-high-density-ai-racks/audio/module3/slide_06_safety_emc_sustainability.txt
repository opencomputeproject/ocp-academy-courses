The compliance section is where Diablo 400 becomes very concrete.

The integrated power rack and its subcomponents must comply with applicable safety standards, including I E C and U L six two three six eight dash one, plus national deviations. AC mains-connected assemblies must withstand an available short-circuit fault current of 10 kiloamps to 40 kiloamps, depending on the application.

Cables rated above 60 volts DC or 42 point 4 volts AC must be U L certified, with a minimum 90 degrees Celsius temperature rating, and suitable for oil-resistant, damp-location, hard-usage conditions. Components and circuits above P S two limits must not be accessible while live. If service access is required, the design needs an interlock, power-off switch, or similar de-energization mechanism.

The specification also calls out creepage and clearance, overcurrent protection, protective earthing and bonding, leakage current limits, fault detection, and rack stability. Leakage current must not exceed 3 point 5 milliamps AC and 10 milliamps DC. The rack must remain stable and not tip over when tilted up to 10 degrees in any direction.

Energy storage systems bring their own safety requirements. Battery modules and cells must comply with applicable standards such as U L nine five four zero, U L nine five four zero A, U L one nine seven three, I E C six two one three three, N F P A eight five five, and transportation requirements.

E M C is also specified. The integrated rack must comply as a Class A device under C I S P R thirty-two and C I S P R thirty-five, plus applicable national regulations. Additional test expectations cover emissions, electrostatic discharge, surge, and radiated immunity.

Finally, the rack, subcomponents, assemblies, and packaging must comply with worldwide environmental, eco-design, and energy-efficiency laws. These requirements are not the glamorous part of Diablo 400, but they are what make a shared architecture buildable.
