Grounding is one of the most consequential choices in a higher voltage DC rack.

Diablo 400 defines two ways to connect the plus-minus 400 volts DC midpoint to protective earth. The first is high resistance midpoint grounding. In this case, the plus-minus 400 volts DC routing uses plus 400 volts DC, midpoint, minus 400 volts DC, and protective earth. The midpoint connects to protective earth through a high resistance path, and the resistor value is design dependent.

The second option is solid contact midpoint grounding. In this case, the midpoint connects to protective earth through a solid connection, or zero-ohm contact.

The appendix lays out the tradeoffs. High resistance grounding can reduce shock risk from touching one pole to ground under normal conditions and can mitigate arcs from a line-to-ground fault. But it does not remove shock risk between plus 400 and minus 400 volts. It also requires effective fault detection, sensor maintenance, and timely response, because a first ground fault may not disrupt operation.

Solid grounding is simpler to design and maintain. It also aligns with clearer safety-standard expectations and can make problem circuits easier to identify. But high-voltage parts must remain inaccessible, arcing may occur until a fuse operates, bonding must carry fault current until protection opens, and multiple solidly grounded power racks can create ground-loop concerns.

The lesson is not that one choice is universally correct. The lesson is that grounding defines fault behavior, protection design, service procedures, and vendor compatibility.
