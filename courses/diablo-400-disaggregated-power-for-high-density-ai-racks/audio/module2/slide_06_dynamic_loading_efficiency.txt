AI loads are dynamic, so Diablo 400 does not stop at steady-state output voltage.

The DC output must stay within its specified undershoot and overshoot limits during transient load changes, with 3 milliseconds settling time. The tests apply with and without capacitive loading on a single unit of 10 millifarads. Battery backup should not trigger during dynamic loading, and AC input current is regulated by power factor correction.

The dynamic load table includes 50 percent, 90 percent, and 140 percent step load changes from a 10 percent minimum load. Those steps can occur up to 10 kilohertz, with a 1 amp per microsecond slew rate, and the maximum undershoot and overshoot is plus or minus 3 percent.

The rack power table captures the short peak behavior expected by different contributors. At 480 volts AC input, the rack average DC load range is 800 kilowatts to one point one megawatts. At 415 volts AC input, it is up to one point one megawatts. At 400 volts AC input, it is up to 718 kilowatts.

Efficiency is measured from AC input at the AC PDU to DC output at the DC PDU, including cooling, distribution, control, and energy or backup-power losses. The minimum values are greater than 95 percent at 20 percent load. At 50 percent load, the requirement is greater than 98 percent for 480 volts AC input and greater than 97 percent for 415 or 400 volts AC input. At full load, the requirement is greater than 97 percent for 480 volts AC input and greater than ninety-six point five percent for 415 or 400 volts AC input.

The specification also leaves room for optional multi-rack parallel operation. In that case, current sharing, droop or active sharing, and synchronized power-up and power-down become part of the rack-level control story.
