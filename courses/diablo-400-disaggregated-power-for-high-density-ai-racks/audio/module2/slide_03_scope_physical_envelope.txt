The Diablo 400 sidecar starts with a practical mechanical constraint: it has to be a power rack that can be built, moved, serviced, and integrated beside high-density IT racks.

The specification gives three representative rack envelopes. The Microsoft configuration lists a height of 2329 millimeters, plus or minus 3 millimeters; a width of 600 millimeters, plus or minus 2 millimeters; and a depth of 1200 millimeters, plus or minus 2 millimeters. The Meta configuration is 2329 millimeters high, 600 millimeters wide, and 1219 millimeters deep. The Google configuration lists 2329 millimeters high, with an option up to 2410 millimeters, 711 millimeters wide, and 1219.2 millimeters deep.

The target loading capacity is 6000 pounds. That matters because the rack may hold power shelves, busbars, battery backup units, capacitor bank units, cabling, protection modules, and structural support.

The mechanical feature list tells you how the rack is meant to be operated. Sliding shelves support push-in and pull-out service for large blocks such as a 4 OU battery backup unit. Blind-mate connectors and static or sliding rails support serviceability. Safety doors, optional locks, intrusion detection, emergency shutoff, AC cable routing, and connectorized options all point to the same idea: the sidecar is not just a box of power electronics. It is a field-serviceable power system.
