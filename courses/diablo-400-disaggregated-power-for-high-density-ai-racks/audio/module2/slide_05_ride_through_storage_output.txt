Diablo 400 separates several ideas that are easy to blur together.

Holdup is the short interval provided by built-in power supply bulk storage. It keeps the rack running while some other form of support engages after AC loss. Ride-through is the ability to keep operating through voltage dropouts, sags, swells, and overvoltage events without interruption or latch-off.

Without energy storage, the minimum holdup requirement is up to 20 milliseconds at 100 percent loading. The specification also allows distributed holdup between the AC to DC power supply in the Diablo 400 rack and the downstream DC to DC converter outside the rack.

With energy storage, the power supply and shelf hold-up energy must cover the smooth transition from AC dropout to energy storage ramp-up. The rack can support battery backup units, or B B Us, and capacitor bank units, or C B Us. If a B B U option is used, battery backup time must support a range of 45 to 90 seconds at 100 percent loading.

On the output side, Diablo 400 defines a nominal plus-minus 400 volts DC architecture. The common wire is the neutral polarity between plus 400 volts DC and minus 400 volts DC. At no load, the table lists plus 410 volts DC to common and minus 410 volts DC to common. At full load, it lists plus 400 volts DC and minus 400 volts DC to common, with plus or minus zero point five percent regulation.

That is the core electrical handoff: the sidecar turns facility AC into a controlled high-voltage DC output for the IT rack power path.
