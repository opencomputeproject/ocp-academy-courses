The LVDC white paper is clear that technology is moving faster than the standards landscape.

The paper identifies existing product-level standards and proven architectures from marine, industrial, electric vehicle, and DC microgrid domains as useful starting points. But data centers are a demanding case. They combine high power, strict uptime expectations, dense white-space equipment, changing utility requirements, and new power-electronic behavior.

The standards gaps are system-level as much as component-level. The industry still needs clearer guidance for complete DC system architecture, coordinated controls, load sharing, fault response, maintenance procedures, AC to DC interface management, and worker protection. DC arc flash is an especially important gap because many existing calculation methods and safety practices were built around AC systems.

The paper points toward practical interim work. Organizations can use existing standards where the fit is reasonable, work with certifying bodies, perform hazard-based risk assessment, use laboratory testing, and model control behavior before field deployment. Hardware-in-the-loop simulations are one tool for validating reliability, controls, and communication before scaling.

For OCP, the future-work agenda is collaborative. Voltage and power-quality alignment need continued refinement. DC arc flash rules need focused industry work. OCP Projects: Power Distribution and Rack and Power need alignment so the handoff from facility distribution to rack power does not become a fragmented boundary.

This is where the optional module connects back to Diablo 400. The sidecar standard gives the industry a concrete rack-level interface. Future LVDC work asks how that interface should evolve into a safe, interoperable, facility-scale DC ecosystem.
