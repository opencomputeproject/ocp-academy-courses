The LVDC future depends on voltage choices that are more subtle than a single headline number.

The white paper anchors low voltage at or below 1000 volts AC or 1500 volts DC. It also distinguishes nominal voltage, rated voltage, and operating voltage. Nominal voltage names the system. Rated voltage belongs to equipment qualification. Operating voltage is what the system actually sees under normal and temporary conditions.

That distinction matters because DC systems can use voltage bands. Active DC microgrids coordinate sources, loads, and prosumers with droop curves, where voltage reflects the balance between supply and demand. Traditional DC microgrids often use wider voltage bands to support interoperability and energy storage behavior. Data center compute loads, by contrast, tend to need tighter voltage control near component limits.

The paper also points to standard LVDC voltage families such as 350 volts, 700 volts, and 1400 volts, plus bipolar notations such as 350 over 700 volts and 700 over 1400 volts. It notes that 400 over 800 volts does not scale cleanly to a 1600 volt system because that would exceed the 1500 volt low-voltage DC boundary.

Topology adds another layer. DC grids may be bipolar or unipolar. They may use T N dash S grounding or I T grounding. Bipolar systems can reduce voltage to earth for a given pole-to-pole power transfer, but they introduce midpoint and protection choices. Unipolar systems are simpler in some ways, but can carry higher voltage-to-earth implications.

So the future LVDC discussion is not just about choosing a voltage. It is about choosing an ecosystem of voltages, bands, topology, earthing, protection, and compatible devices.
