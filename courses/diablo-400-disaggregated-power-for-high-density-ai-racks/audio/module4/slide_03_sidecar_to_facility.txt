Diablo 400 focuses on the rack-level sidecar, but the LVDC white paper places that sidecar inside a broader facility transition.

The paper defines low voltage direct current, or LVDC, power distribution as 1500 volts DC or below. In the data center context, LVDC becomes the interface between incoming medium voltage AC power in the gray space and AI compute loads in the white space. That framing matters because it moves the discussion from a single rack to the whole path from grid, local generation, or energy storage into the compute environment.

The white paper also describes why the sidecar is a practical first step. Initial Diablo-style power racks let ORV three HPR-based IT racks move from AC-supplied 33 kilowatt or 72 kilowatt power shelves toward DC-supplied 100 kilowatt power shelves in the same one OU footprint. The paper calls that a 39 percent increase in power density per power shelf. It also notes that moving in-rack energy storage out to the power rack can free roughly 12 to 16 OU in systems that use in-rack storage.

From there, the paper describes a staged path. Stage 1 supplies IT racks, and optionally associated cooling, with DC while other equipment remains on AC. Stage 2 extends DC to a larger IT module, including associated cooling devices. Stage 3 imagines full LVDC distribution between medium voltage input and all data center equipment.

That is the bigger idea: Diablo solves an urgent problem with a sidecar, while LVDC asks how much of the facility power chain should eventually become DC-native.
