The starting point for Diablo 400 is the speed at which AI infrastructure is changing the power profile of a rack.

Traditional cloud compute and storage racks have often lived below 20 kilowatts. Earlier high-power rack work pushed that much higher, with 48 volt distribution helping the industry move from the tens of kilowatts into the 100 kilowatt range. But AI systems are now driving a different class of problem. Accelerator count, accelerator power, network density, memory bandwidth, and liquid cooling all push more useful compute into the same physical envelope.

The Diablo 400 specification names this clearly. It points to increased rack power, moving from 33 kilowatts to hundreds of kilowatts, larger rack and pod scale, and significant challenges around interconnect density, power delivery, and overall system integration.

That is the key. The power architecture is not changing because the industry wants a new cabinet shape. It is changing because the compute rack has become too valuable to fill with power conversion and backup hardware that could be located somewhere else. A disaggregated power rack lets the IT rack concentrate on compute, networking, and cooling, while the adjacent power rack handles conversion, distribution, and energy support.
