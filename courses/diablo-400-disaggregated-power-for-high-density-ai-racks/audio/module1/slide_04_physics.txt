The basic physics behind the shift is simple, but the consequences are large.

For a fixed amount of power, voltage and current trade off against each other. If voltage goes up, current goes down. That matters because conductor loss is proportional to current squared times resistance. Engineers often describe this as I squared R loss. When current is high, the heat generated in cables and busbars climbs very quickly.

At low voltage, a very high-power rack needs extremely high current. That means heavier copper, more heat, larger conductors, more difficult routing, and less room for compute infrastructure. Raising the distribution voltage reduces the current required for the same power level. The system does not keep the same copper and magically collect all the theoretical loss reduction. Designers usually spend part of that gain by reducing conductor size, simplifying routing, and fitting the power path into a more practical mechanical envelope.

This is why plus-minus 400 volt and 800 volt class discussions show up repeatedly in the Diablo 400 ecosystem. The point is not the number by itself. The point is a power architecture that can support hundreds of kilowatts per IT rack, and eventually 1 megawatt class deployment, without making current, copper, thermal stress, and serviceability the dominant constraints.
