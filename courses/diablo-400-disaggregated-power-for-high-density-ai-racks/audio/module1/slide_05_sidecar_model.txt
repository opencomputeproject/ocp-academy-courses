Diablo 400 uses a disaggregated power rack, also called a sidecar, to separate power infrastructure from the IT rack.

In a conventional rack power model, power conversion and backup elements can consume space inside the compute rack. That is acceptable while rack power is modest. It becomes a serious tradeoff when accelerators, switch trays, manifolds, cold plates, and high-speed fabrics are all competing for space. Diablo 400 changes the placement of the power system. The sidecar receives three-phase AC input, converts and distributes high-voltage DC output, and supports energy storage options such as battery backup units and capacitor bank units.

That shift has several practical benefits. The IT rack can preserve more space for xPUs, networking, and cooling. Power hardware can be arranged in a rack optimized for service, safety, cable routing, and airflow. Energy storage can be scaled or configured based on the platform and operator need. And a common sidecar interface creates room for a broader supplier ecosystem, because the interface between the power rack and the IT rack becomes something the industry can standardize.

In short, Diablo 400 is not just a voltage change. It is a placement, interface, and serviceability change.
